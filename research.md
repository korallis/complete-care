# UK care management software has a children's homes–shaped hole

**No leading UK care management platform genuinely serves all three of domiciliary care, supported living, and children's residential care.** This gap — particularly the near-total absence of purpose-built children's homes functionality — represents the single largest positioning opportunity in UK care tech today. After analysing the five most relevant competitors (Birdie, Log My Care, Person Centred Software, Nourish Care, and PASS by everyLIFE), a consistent pattern emerges: every platform was built for adult social care first, and children's homes remain either completely ignored or treated as an afterthought with no specialist features, no Ofsted compliance tools, and no visible customer base. A new entrant that nails the intersection of these three care domains could own a market nobody is fighting for.

## Birdie: the dom care purist that can't cross over

**What they do best.** Birdie is the most polished, purpose-built domiciliary care platform on the market. Built from scratch as a unified system (not stitched-together acquisitions), it delivers care planning, rostering, finance, and auditing in a single workflow where visit data flows automatically into invoices. The proprietary **Q-Score** maps real-time operational data against CQC Key Lines of Enquiry, giving managers a live compliance dashboard. Birdie is also the only bundled UK care platform with an **open API**, enabling integrations with external CRMs, learning management systems, and applicant tracking tools. With **$58–62 million in VC funding**, 1,000+ provider partners, and 60 million visits per year, it has meaningful scale and momentum. A 4.5-star Trustpilot rating across 386 reviews reflects genuinely strong user satisfaction among frontline carers and managers.

**Where they are weak.** Birdie's singular focus is simultaneously its ceiling. The platform serves **domiciliary care only** — no residential, no nursing, no supported living, no children's homes. Providers operating across multiple care types cannot use Birdie as their single system. Rostering remains a pain point for managers: users report they cannot drag-and-drop runs, the system doesn't auto-total weekly carer hours, and reassigning shifts requires a clunky unassign-then-reassign workflow. There is **no phone support** at any standard tier — chat and email only — and multiple 1-star Trustpilot reviews cite frustration at being unable to speak to a human. One reviewer wrote: *"You can never speak to anyone… once they take the money it's all on text."* Contract flexibility is also a concern, with at least one user reporting difficulty exiting their agreement.

**What audience they ignore.** Any provider outside adult domiciliary care is invisible to Birdie. Supported living users must rely on workarounds (creating "clients" for properties rather than individuals, using unscheduled visits for ad-hoc support). Children's homes are completely unserved — every piece of Birdie's language, design, and compliance tooling references "older adults" and "ageing at home." There are no Ofsted tools, no children's safeguarding features, and no content acknowledging this care type exists.

**The positioning gap.** Birdie's strategy is geographic expansion (Spain, Ireland, France) rather than vertical expansion. This means any UK provider with mixed services — say, a group running both home care and supported living — must either use Birdie alongside a second system or choose a competitor. For the growing number of care organisations diversifying across service types, Birdie is structurally unable to be their primary platform.

## Log My Care: beloved by small LD providers, unproven everywhere else

**What they do best.** Log My Care has the most intuitive interface in the sector. A **4.7/5 Google rating across 348 reviews** — the highest of any competitor — reflects an app that carers can genuinely use without training. Their free Starter tier (for providers with fewer than 10 service users) creates a low-friction entry point that no competitor matches. The platform's roots in **learning disability care** give it natural strength in supported living, with features like PBS (Positive Behaviour Support) plans, outcomes and goals tracking, and offline mode for community outings. Per-service-user pricing is published transparently, and contracts run month-to-month with no long-term lock-in — a sharp contrast to competitors demanding 12-month commitments.

**Where they are weak.** Log My Care's dom care offering is new and underdeveloped. Rostering was only recently built, and some features (like open-visit posting) are still marked "in development." The platform has **no billing, invoicing, or financial management** capabilities — a critical gap for home care agencies that need end-to-end operational software. Integration options are extremely limited (PainChek, GP Connect, London Care Record — that's essentially it), compared to competitors with broader ecosystems. Perhaps most concerning, Trustpilot reviews (albeit only three) paint a picture of poor post-sale support: *"No-one calls, no-one writes, no-one cares. You cannot speak with anyone on the phone."* The free tier, while attractive, is functionally skeletal — it lacks care plans, incident management, body maps, and risk assessments.

**What audience they ignore.** Children's homes are entirely absent from Log My Care's proposition. The platform lists its care types as learning disabilities, domiciliary care, residential (adult), supported living, and nursing — no children's services. There are no Ofsted compliance features. Larger care groups needing enterprise-grade analytics, multi-system integrations, or sophisticated finance tools will also find Log My Care too lightweight. The modular add-on pricing (rostering +£210/month, eMAR +£150/month, family portal +£100/month) means total costs for a 50-bed service can reach **£12,720/year** — more than some all-in-one competitors.

**The positioning gap.** Log My Care occupies a "simple and affordable" niche that works brilliantly for small LD/supported living providers but doesn't scale well. They lack the operational depth (finance, HR, CRM) that growing agencies need, and their dom care features trail purpose-built competitors by years. The gap between their excellent UX reputation and their thin feature set creates a window for a competitor that combines Log My Care–level simplicity with genuine operational completeness.

## Person Centred Software: the residential care giant struggling to diversify

**What they do best.** PCS is the UK's largest care management software provider by sheer installed base: **8,400+ care homes** use their mCare platform, with 13 million care notes created daily and 179 billion annual care observations processed. Their CQC compliance track record is formidable — **94–95% of CQC-inspected homes using mCare are rated Good or Outstanding**. The acquisition spree (ATLAS eMAR, Oomph! wellbeing, Redcrier training, KareInn care planning) has created the broadest product suite in the market. Their new IQ benchmarking platform, powered by what may be the largest structured dataset in UK social care, represents a genuine emerging moat. Enterprise customers include Barchester, Sanctuary Care, the Salvation Army, and NHS England.

**Where they are weak.** PCS's dominance in residential care has not translated to other settings. **Domiciliary care** has a product page but no visible rostering, scheduling, travel time management, GPS tracking, or electronic visit verification — the fundamental features home care agencies need. Key mobile features including body maps and incident reports are **desktop-only**, making the platform impractical for community-based carers. Pricing is completely opaque and positioned at the premium end; a Glassdoor employee review revealed an internal culture where senior leaders have reportedly said: *"Do you know how much care homes charge? They can afford these prices."* A 12-month cancellation notice requirement and **2.9/5 Glassdoor employer rating** (only 40% of employees recommend working there) suggest internal and commercial friction. Multiple named customers have publicly switched away to competitors like Log My Care, citing cost and inflexibility.

**What audience they ignore.** PCS had **zero native children's homes capability** until a December 2025 strategic partnership with Clearcare (800+ children's care locations). Even now, Clearcare operates as a completely separate product with its own team, codebase, and Ofsted compliance tooling — it is not integrated into mCare. Supported living is listed on partner pages but absent from PCS's main website navigation, with no PBS workflows, outcomes tracking, or tenancy-aware documentation. Small and medium providers are effectively priced out.

**The positioning gap.** PCS's acquisition-led growth creates a "stitched together" platform risk. Five acquisitions plus the Clearcare partnership since 2023 add breadth on paper but integration complexity in practice. The Clearcare deal specifically signals that PCS recognises the children's homes gap but chose to buy rather than build — meaning the user experience for a provider running both adult residential and children's homes under PCS will involve two entirely separate systems. A truly unified platform serving both audiences would hold a structural advantage.

## Nourish Care: the quiet acquirer with a CarePlanner integration headache

**What they do best.** Nourish has assembled the broadest care domain coverage of any competitor through its **August 2023 acquisition of CarePlanner** (2,000+ dom care agencies). The combined entity supports **400,000+ people** across residential homes, nursing homes, domiciliary care, and supported living — making it arguably the UK's largest care software provider by service user count. Regulatory credentials are strong: Nourish was among the first NHS Assured DSCR suppliers and the first PRSB Quality Partner. A standout statistic: **78% of services rated "Requires Improvement" improved to "Good" within 12 months** of adopting Nourish. The platform's care planning templates and person-centred timelines are genuinely deep for residential settings, and the Milestones Trust partnership demonstrates credibility in learning disability and supported living contexts.

**Where they are weak.** The CarePlanner integration is still in progress, and the transition has generated friction. Trustpilot carries a **3.4/5 rating across 671 reviews** — the lowest of the competitors with meaningful review volume. Recent 1-star reviews (November–December 2025) consistently cite **unresponsive customer support and aggressive contract lock-in**: *"Their customer support feels practically nonexistent — slow responses, vague answers… the contract process feels designed to lock you in while giving you as little support as possible."* Clinical users in nursing homes find the platform overly complex: one nurse wrote, *"It is a truly awful care plan platform especially for nursing homes with complex needs… give me PCS any day."* Pricing is opaque and reportedly **up to 50% more expensive** than alternatives like Log My Care, with additional setup fees. Trustpilot notes that Nourish *"hasn't replied to negative reviews"* — a basic customer service failure.

**What audience they ignore.** Despite listing "children and young people's services" as a care type, Nourish has **no dedicated children's homes product page, no case studies, no Ofsted-specific templates, and no visible children's homes customers**. This is a theoretical capability rather than a proven one. Smaller providers without dedicated admin teams find the platform's complexity and training requirements (2+ days for senior staff) prohibitive. The lack of a free tier or even transparent pricing makes Nourish invisible to new and micro providers.

**The positioning gap.** Nourish's breadth-through-acquisition strategy means it technically covers more care types than any single competitor, but the depth in each domain varies wildly. Dom care features are mature (inherited from CarePlanner) but the two platforms haven't fully unified. Residential care is strong but facing UX criticism. Supported living is served but secondary. Children's homes are a checkbox, not a capability. A competitor that delivers genuine depth across dom care, supported living, and children's homes — rather than breadth achieved by bolting acquisitions together — could position itself as the "built right" alternative.

## PASS by everyLIFE: the compliance-first all-in-one with app reliability issues

**What they do best.** PASS's standout positioning is its all-inclusive pricing model: care planning, rostering, eMAR, finance/invoicing, reporting, GP Connect, and the family portal (openPASS) all come under a **single licence with no bolt-on fees**. This is a genuine commercial advantage over competitors like Nourish and Access Group, where rostering, eMAR, and invoicing are separate paid modules. PASS was the **first and only** NHS Assured Solution to meet all 14 DSCR standards — a compliance credential no competitor can match. The recently launched **PASSgenius AI suite** (care note summaries, automatic risk flags, capacity heatmaps, travel optimisation) is included at no extra cost and represents meaningful innovation. Full offline mobile functionality and 24/7 UK-based support (phone answered in under 1 minute) round out a strong operational package. PASS users are reportedly **37% more likely to be rated Outstanding by CQC** than the national average.

**Where they are weak.** The mobile app is PASS's Achilles heel. Apple App Store reviews from frontline carers describe persistent **stability and reliability issues**: *"It used to be good, now it is awful. Signing-in issues, calls un-allocating themselves, cancelled calls reinstating themselves… I am in constant contact with customer service, and nothing is ever done."* Another carer reported that app connectivity failures led to their employer accusing them of fraud. Updates frequently introduce new bugs. PASS has **no published third-party integrations** beyond GP Connect and its PASSport API — a closed ecosystem compared to Birdie's open API approach. Pricing is not published, and the 12-month minimum contract echoes competitor weaknesses. The last known funding round was **2017**, raising questions about investment capacity compared to the $60M+ Birdie has raised. PASS has negligible presence on major review platforms (Capterra, G2, Trustpilot), which limits social proof.

**What audience they ignore.** PASS mentions "children's care" in a single line on its solutions overview page (*"PASS optimises the care for children by offering adaptable care plans"*) but has **no dedicated children's homes product page, no Ofsted compliance features, no children's safeguarding tools, and no children's homes case studies**. The mobile app listing describes the product as for "residential care, supported living, nursing care, complex care" — children's care isn't mentioned. Providers seeking specialist children's residential software will find nothing of substance.

**The positioning gap.** PASS's all-in-one pricing and compliance leadership should make it a market leader, but app quality undermines the proposition at the point of care delivery — literally in carers' hands. The company's limited funding runway (vs. Birdie) constrains its ability to invest in the engineering resources needed to fix reliability issues and expand into new verticals. Children's homes remain unserved despite the claim, and the closed integration ecosystem limits appeal to tech-savvy providers building multi-tool stacks.

## How a new UK care CRM could dominate this space

The competitive analysis reveals five exploitable gaps that, taken together, define a clear positioning strategy for a new entrant.

**Own the children's homes vertical from day one.** This is the single most underserved segment in UK care tech. Not one of the five leading platforms has purpose-built Ofsted compliance tools, Regulation 44/45 visit templates, children's safeguarding workflows, educational progress tracking, age-appropriate outcome measurement, or LAC (Looked After Children) process support. Specialist players like OVCare and Clearcare exist but operate as narrow point solutions, not full care management platforms. Building **native Ofsted compliance alongside CQC compliance** in a single system would immediately differentiate from every competitor. The children's homes market is growing (there were over 2,900 children's homes in England as of recent Ofsted data) and providers in this space are desperate for modern software that understands their regulatory reality.

**Build the only platform purpose-designed for the dom care + supported living + children's homes triangle.** Care organisations increasingly operate across multiple settings. A group might run domiciliary care for elderly adults, supported living for adults with learning disabilities, and a children's home under Ofsted registration — all under one CQC/Ofsted-registered entity. Today, this provider needs two or three separate software systems. The new entrant should position itself as **"one platform, three care worlds"** with domain-specific workflows, compliance tooling, and terminology that adapts to each setting while sharing a unified data layer, staff management system, and reporting dashboard. This directly exploits Birdie's single-domain limitation, PCS's residential-only depth, and every competitor's failure to meaningfully serve children's homes.

**Lead with radical pricing transparency and flexible contracts.** Four of five competitors hide their pricing behind sales calls. Nourish and PCS require 12-month minimum contracts and charge opaque setup fees. The new entrant should publish **all pricing on its website** (following Birdie and Log My Care's lead), offer genuine month-to-month contracts with no lock-in, and include a meaningful free tier for micro-providers (improving on Log My Care's skeletal Starter plan by including care plans and basic compliance tools). This transparency strategy builds trust in a sector where care providers — often small businesses with tight margins — deeply resent being locked into contracts with deteriorating support.

**Solve the mobile reliability problem that PASS can't.** Frontline carers live in the mobile app. PASS's app reliability issues and PCS's desktop-only features for key functions (body maps, incident reports) create an opening for a platform that treats mobile as a **first-class citizen with obsessive attention to offline capability, stability, and speed**. Voice-to-text care notes, one-tap incident logging, photo/video attachments, and offline-first architecture (not offline-as-afterthought) should be core design principles. Every hour a carer spends fighting unreliable software is an hour not spent caring.

**Combine Log My Care's UX simplicity with Birdie's operational depth.** The market currently forces providers to choose between "simple but limited" (Log My Care) and "powerful but complex" (Nourish, PCS). The winning new entrant would offer a **progressively disclosed interface** — simple enough that a new carer can log notes within minutes of their first shift, but with deep operational layers (rostering, finance, invoicing, HR, CRM, analytics) that managers and directors unlock as their organisation grows. Log My Care proves that exceptional UX wins hearts; Birdie proves that operational completeness wins contracts. The new platform needs both.

**Practical launch sequencing matters.** The most defensible go-to-market is to launch with children's homes first (uncontested territory, smaller market but zero competition for purpose-built solutions), expand into supported living second (natural adjacency, shared safeguarding DNA), and enter domiciliary care third (largest market but most competitive). Each expansion should bring domain-specific compliance, workflows, and templates — not generic care plans relabelled for different settings. Early children's homes customers become case studies and reference accounts that no competitor can counter, because no competitor has them.

## Conclusion

The UK care management software market is simultaneously crowded and full of gaps. The crowd is in adult domiciliary care and residential homes, where Birdie, PCS, Nourish, and PASS compete fiercely. The gap is in children's residential care — a regulated, growing sector with **zero purpose-built modern platforms** from any leading vendor. Supported living sits somewhere in between: partially served by several competitors but rarely with genuine depth. The new entrant that builds native Ofsted + CQC compliance into a single, mobile-first, transparently priced platform covering all three domains will not be competing with incumbents — it will be creating a category they cannot easily follow, because their architectures, business models, and institutional DNA are all anchored in adult care. The window is open now, before any incumbent decides to build rather than acquire its way into children's services.
